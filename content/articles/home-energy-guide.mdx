## Where Does Home Energy Go?

Your home is quietly one of the biggest contributors to your carbon footprint. The average American household produces about **3.5 tons of CO2 per year** just from home energy use. But where does it all go?

### Heating & Cooling: The Big One

Nearly half of home energy goes to heating and cooling. If you live in a cold climate with a natural gas furnace, this alone can account for 2+ tons of CO2 annually. Air conditioning in hot climates is also significant, though typically less carbon-intensive since it runs on electricity.

### Hot Water

Water heating is the second-largest energy expense in most homes, accounting for about 18% of your energy bill. Old tank-style water heaters keep 40-80 gallons hot 24/7, even when you're asleep or at work.

### Appliances & Electronics

Your refrigerator, washer, dryer, dishwasher, and all those plugged-in devices add up. "Phantom loads" -- devices that draw power even when off -- can account for 5-10% of your electricity bill.

### Lighting

While a smaller slice of the pie thanks to LED adoption, lighting still matters, especially if you haven't switched from incandescent or CFL bulbs yet.

## The Grid Matters More Than You Think

Here's something most people don't realize: **where you live dramatically changes your electricity's carbon intensity**. A kilowatt-hour of electricity in West Virginia (mostly coal) produces roughly 10 times more CO2 than the same kilowatt-hour in Vermont (mostly hydro and nuclear).

This means the same electric heater can be very clean or very dirty depending on your local grid mix.

## 5 Easy Wins

Here are five changes ranked by impact and effort:

### 1. Adjust Your Thermostat

Turning your thermostat down 2 degrees in winter (and up 2 degrees in summer) can save **5-10% on heating and cooling costs** with almost zero effort. A programmable or smart thermostat makes this automatic.

### 2. Switch to LED Bulbs

If you haven't already, replace remaining incandescent bulbs with LEDs. They use 75% less energy and last 25 times longer. This is the easiest win on the list.

### 3. Seal Air Leaks

Drafty windows and doors can waste 25-30% of your heating and cooling energy. Weatherstripping and caulk cost under $20 and take an afternoon to apply.

### 4. Wash Clothes in Cold Water

About 90% of the energy your washing machine uses goes to heating water. Cold water cleans just as well for most loads and can save **~0.1 tons of CO2 per year**.

### 5. Switch to a Green Energy Plan

Many utilities now offer renewable energy options. Even if your grid is dirty, you can often choose a 100% renewable plan for just a few dollars more per month. This single switch can eliminate your electricity emissions entirely.

## The Bigger Moves

When you're ready for higher-impact changes, consider a heat pump (replaces both furnace and AC with a unit 3-4x more efficient), a heat pump water heater, or rooftop solar. These require upfront investment but pay for themselves over time -- and often qualify for significant tax credits.
