## Transportation Is Your Biggest Lever

For most Americans, transportation is the **single largest source of carbon emissions**, accounting for about 4.6 tons of CO2 per year. And the majority of that comes from one thing: your car.

The average American drives about 13,500 miles per year. If you drive a typical gasoline car getting 25 MPG, that's roughly **5.4 tons of CO2** just from driving (including fuel production emissions).

## How Different Modes Compare

Not all ways of getting around are equal. Here's the CO2 per passenger-mile for common transport modes:

- **Single-occupancy car (gasoline):** 0.89 lbs CO2/mile
- **Ride-share/carpool (2 people):** 0.45 lbs CO2/mile
- **Bus (urban):** 0.33 lbs CO2/mile
- **Commuter rail:** 0.22 lbs CO2/mile
- **Subway/metro:** 0.17 lbs CO2/mile
- **Electric car:** 0.15-0.35 lbs CO2/mile (varies by grid)
- **E-bike:** 0.03 lbs CO2/mile
- **Bicycle:** 0 lbs CO2/mile

## The Remote Work Effect

If the pandemic taught us anything about emissions, it's that **not commuting is the cleanest commute**. Working from home even 2-3 days per week can eliminate 1-2 tons of CO2 annually for the average commuter.

Even hybrid work makes a big difference. One study found that employees who worked from home 2 days per week reduced their commuting emissions by roughly 40%.

## The EV Question

Electric vehicles are significantly cleaner than gas cars, but the exact benefit depends on your electricity grid:

- **Clean grid (hydro/wind/solar):** An EV produces 70-80% fewer emissions than a gas car
- **Average US grid:** An EV produces about 50-60% fewer emissions
- **Coal-heavy grid:** An EV still produces 20-30% fewer emissions

The manufacturing of EV batteries does create upfront emissions, but most studies show that EVs "pay back" their manufacturing carbon debt within 1-2 years of driving.

If a new EV isn't in your budget, a **used hybrid** or simply driving your current car less can be equally impactful.

## Practical Moves

### Combine and Eliminate Trips

Before thinking about how you travel, think about whether you need to travel. Combining errands into one trip and eliminating unnecessary drives can cut your driving emissions by 10-20%.

### Try Transit for Your Commute

Even switching to public transit two days a week can save **0.5-1.0 tons of CO2 per year**, depending on your commute distance. Many cities offer monthly passes that make this cost-effective too.

### Consider an E-Bike

For trips under 5 miles, an e-bike is faster than driving (when you factor in parking), costs pennies to charge, and produces virtually zero emissions. They're one of the most underrated climate solutions available today.

### Fly Less, Fly Smarter

A single round-trip cross-country flight produces about 1 ton of CO2 per passenger. If you fly frequently, reducing one flight per year can be one of the single biggest emission cuts available to you. When you do fly, direct flights are significantly more efficient than connecting flights.

## The Big Picture

You don't need to sell your car tomorrow. But understanding that transportation is likely your biggest emission category means small changes here have outsized impact. Start with the easiest swap -- whether that's working from home one more day or biking to the grocery store -- and build from there.
