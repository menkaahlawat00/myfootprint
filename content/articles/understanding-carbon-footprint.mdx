## The Short Version

A **carbon footprint** is the total amount of greenhouse gases your lifestyle generates, measured in tons of CO2 equivalent per year. Think of it as the invisible exhaust trail your daily life leaves behind -- every meal, commute, online order, and Netflix binge contributes.

## Why Should You Care?

Earth's climate is changing because we're pumping too much CO2 (and other greenhouse gases like methane) into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat, warming the planet and destabilizing weather patterns, ecosystems, and food supplies.

The good news? Understanding your footprint is the first step toward shrinking it.

## The Average American's Footprint

The average American produces roughly **16 tons of CO2 per year**. That's more than double the global average of about 6.3 tons, and roughly four times what scientists say is sustainable (around 4 tons per person by 2050).

Here's roughly how those 16 tons break down:

- **Transportation:** ~4.6 tons (29%)
- **Home energy:** ~3.5 tons (22%)
- **Food:** ~3.3 tons (21%)
- **Goods & services:** ~2.9 tons (18%)
- **Other:** ~1.7 tons (10%)

## But Does Individual Action Even Matter?

This is the big question, and the answer is: **yes, but not in isolation**.

Individual changes do three powerful things:

1. **They reduce demand.** When you buy less meat, fly less, or switch to clean energy, you're sending market signals that shift entire industries.

2. **They shift norms.** When your friends see you biking to work or eating plant-based meals, it normalizes those choices. Social influence is one of the most powerful drivers of behavior change.

3. **They build political will.** People who understand their own footprint are more likely to support systemic changes like carbon pricing, renewable energy mandates, and public transit funding.

No one person can solve climate change alone. But collective individual action, combined with systemic change, is exactly how we get there.
