## Food's Climate Footprint

The global food system is responsible for roughly **one-third of all greenhouse gas emissions**. That includes growing, processing, transporting, refrigerating, and disposing of food. For the average American, food accounts for about 3.3 tons of CO2 per year.

But not all foods are created equal.

## The Protein Problem

When it comes to climate impact, the single biggest factor on your plate is **what kind of protein you eat**.

Here's a rough comparison of CO2 equivalent per kilogram of food produced:

- **Beef:** 27 kg CO2e per kg
- **Cheese:** 13.5 kg CO2e per kg
- **Pork:** 12 kg CO2e per kg
- **Chicken:** 6.9 kg CO2e per kg
- **Eggs:** 4.8 kg CO2e per kg
- **Tofu:** 3.2 kg CO2e per kg
- **Beans/lentils:** 0.9 kg CO2e per kg

Beef produces **30 times more emissions** than beans for the same amount of protein. This is largely because cattle require enormous amounts of land, water, and feed, and they produce methane -- a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO2 over 20 years.

## The Food Waste Factor

Here's a stat that might surprise you: **30-40% of food produced in the US is never eaten**. It ends up in landfills, where it decomposes and produces methane.

The average American household throws away about $1,500 worth of food per year. That wasted food has a carbon footprint of roughly 0.5 tons of CO2 annually -- before it even reaches the landfill.

## Realistic Dietary Shifts

You don't have to go vegan to make a meaningful difference. Here are practical shifts ordered from easiest to most impactful:

### Start with Swaps, Not Sacrifices

Replace beef with chicken in just two meals per week and you'll save roughly **0.3 tons of CO2 per year**. That's the equivalent of driving 750 fewer miles.

### Try "Reducetarian" Eating

Pick one or two days per week to eat plant-based meals. Meatless Mondays alone can reduce your food footprint by about 10%.

### Cut Food Waste

Plan your meals, use your freezer, and learn the difference between "sell by" and "use by" dates. Composting what you can't eat keeps it out of the landfill and eliminates the methane problem.

### Buy Local and Seasonal When You Can

Transportation is a relatively small part of food's footprint (about 6%), but local and seasonal food tends to be fresher, less processed, and less likely to go to waste.

## The Bottom Line

Food is personal, cultural, and emotional -- and nobody should feel guilt about what they eat. But small, sustainable shifts in how we eat can collectively remove millions of tons of CO2 from the atmosphere. The best diet for the planet is one you can actually stick with.
